FlashTalk

A UX/UI Case Study

Overview

Users need an app that works on the go, so they can fit daily learning into their busy work schedules. The biggest issue is lack of time. They need an app which is quick and easy to use.

The Goal

To design a mobile app which bridges social and professional language barriers, helps users to achieve language competence, and function confidently in a business environment.

Project Details

  • Role: Research, Interviews, Wireframing & User Testing
  • Timeline: 3 weeks
  • Tools: Balsamiq, Marvel, Adobe XD

Competitive Analysis

Quizlet was the only app which allowed for sharing cards/sets, which was a feature I found very useful from a student's perspective, who has tons of new vocabulary every week. Sharing meant users could practice using friends vocabulary sets, making learning quicker and more enjoyable. I loved the praise Duolingo gives its users, "Haha, I'm excellent". Memrise focuses on the context of the language or 'real-life language', and the importance of gaining 'the confidence to speak.' Speaking and confidence kept coming up during interviews and discussions. I drew on features from these apps in my design process.

Interviews

I found the interview process very informative. It gave me insight into how my target audience felt about learning new vocabulary, how and why they learned, and what was missing from the learning material they had used.

User Personas

These users represent a typical user of FlashTalk, their goals, needs and pain points are constructed from the interviews and discussions I had with potential users.

User Flows

These user flows show the path for new users to create their profiles, and save a new vocabulary card. It also shows the path existing users would take to edit their profiles and save a new card. Creating a profile was important because because I felt a flashcard app, which I wanted users to use regularly, would require this step. Adding and saving new cards was one of the main app features, which I wanted to map out and use for usability testing at a later stage of my process.

Insight after testing

  1. Onboarding should be shorter, give users a 'skip' or 'maybe later' option.
  2. Add text labels to icons to make them more intuitive.
  3. Use a conversational tone when creating content.

Onboarding

The feedback I received after testing my low fidelity prototype revealed that users felt the onboarding process was too long, and "too much like a survey". I decided to incorporate the onboarding onto the sign up screen, so as to make the onboarding process quicker, smoother and more pleasant for the user.

Next Steps

  1. Design a high fidelity prototype and conduct usability testing.
  2. Add share card and share set features.
  3. Test the onboarding flow.

Lessons Learned

Giving users what they're familiar with, makes them feel comfortable, not bored. Analysing data from interviews and creating a persona is vital to the design process. Sketches allow me to focus on the user journey rather than the visual elements of the design.
