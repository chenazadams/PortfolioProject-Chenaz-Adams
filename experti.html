ExpertI

A UX/UI Case Study

Overview

Expert-I, a CareerFoundry student project, is an online expert advice service, which provides users with access to and the opportunity to connect with experts in a variety of fields, from the comfort of their own homes, and on-the-go.

The Problem

Expert advice is expensive and not readily available and accessible to all. Many users lack the confidence and resources to verify the validity of advice. Users are reluctant to pay for online advice and prefer online communities.

Project Details

  • Duration: 5 months
  • My Role: research, wireframing, usability testing, prototypes, UI design
  • Tools: Adobe XD, Usability Hub, Overflow
  • Product: high fidelity interactive prototype

Competitive Analysis

SWOT

Initially, I struggled to find competitors who offered a service, which provided advice in a variety of fields. I found I could gain insight into online advice presence from looking at paid (JustAnswer) and free services (Quora). Learning from the features they offered, their market presence and client base. As I progressed I found features which I felt would work well with Expert-I, for example call credits, which OnCall uses. In my interviews, I saw that potential customer really liked the idea of uploading credits.

Surveys & Interviews

33 potential users were recruited from my personal network using Slack and WhatsApp. Surveys were conducted and analysed using Google Forms. Interviews were conducted online using Skype, with 5 participants, which I recruited on Slack. Interviews lasted between 20 and 40 minutes.

User Persona

I found user personas very useful throughout the design process. I created 3 personas, an example of which, is seen below. I referred to their quotes and pain points to help me stay on track, and remain consistent in my design choices.

User Flows

User flows enabled me to visualise the search, booking, and payment processes of Expert-I more clearly and concisely. Breaking the process up into sections made design decisions easier and more manageable.

Card Sorting

I conducted a hybrid card sort with 10 participants, 24 cards using OptimalSort, with 3 defined categories. Participants were asked to create and name the missing categories, on average 4 new categories were created per participant. I felt that a hybrid card sort would leave enough flexibility for users to group things according to what they felt was intuitive, familiar, and easy to follow.

3D Cluster View

I’ve included this analysis because I feel that it shows the card clusters or groups clearly. Some of the results aren’t surprising, for eg., all the cards with the word ‘Call’ on them are grouped together, and all the ones with ‘Expert’ are grouped together. I predicted this, and that’s the reason I chose a hybrid card sort. Forcing users to create their own categories. It also shows the cards which users weren’t sure of, for eg. ‘Favorites’ and ‘Special Offers’ and 'Getting Started'.

Project Takeaways

UX Takeaways

  • Design tool practice
  • Analysing feedback
  • Keep the user persona in mind while iterating and ideating
  • Accessibility parameters when selecting colors, fonts and icons
  • The importance of contrast for legibility and accessibility

Business Takeaways

  • Take into account the business implications of design decisions throughout the design process, for eg. When and how to introduce new users to special offers and call rates.
  • Consider a possible payment strategy, for eg. Would chats cost less than video calls?
  • What are the financial implications of offering every new user a free call? Would experts be willing to donate their time as part of the vetting process?

Next Steps

  • Re-test the prototype and make necessary changes based on feedback.
  • Do a round of guerrilla testing to test the payment flow.
  • Interview experts and ask whether they'd be prepared to donate a 30 minute online consultation during the current pandemic.
